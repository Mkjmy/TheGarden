GARDEN / COMMUNITY GUIDELINES (NAP)

FEED PRINCIPLES NAP

The Core Principle

We believe in a space free from coercion. Our goal is to ensure every interaction is based on mutual respect and voluntary consent.

We aim to resolve conflicts through dialogue, reserving protective actions for direct self-defense against aggression.

1. Physical Safety
Physical Integrity We value a space where no one fears physical harm or unwanted contact.
Credible Threats Safety is compromised when threats are specific, plausible, and demonstrate clear intent.
Respect for Property Unauthorized use or intentional damage to another’s property disrupts the stability of our community.
2. Respect for Ownership
Voluntary Sharing Using or taking resources should always be based on clear and informed agreement.
3. Honesty and Trust
The Importance of Truth Manipulation or the omission of critical facts undermines the trust that makes this community possible.

We believe honesty is the foundation of genuine consent.

4. Freedom from Intimidation
Discouraging Pressure We encourage dialogue that is free from threats of reputational or material harm.
Privacy and Respect Threatening to expose private information to force behavior is contrary to our shared values.
5. Respectful Interaction
Healthy Boundaries We hope to avoid repeated, targeted behavior that restricts a person’s ability to participate comfortably.
  • Respect individual space
  • Value diverse perspectives
  • Engage in healthy disagreement

We distinguish between thoughtful criticism and targeted hostility.

6. Shared Understanding
Self-Defense We respect the right to take proportionate action to protect oneself.
Voluntary Association Our community is built on interactions that everyone chooses to be part of.
Collective Obligation No individual is inherently bound by an obligation to a group or society that they did not voluntarily choose to join.
Personal Limits We respect that each individual has their own capacity to help or engage.
7. Boundaries & Responsibility

Environment: We consider the indirect impact of our actions on the shared environment as part of our responsibility.

Information: We value the free exchange of ideas while respecting voluntary agreements between individuals.

Expression: We encourage open and even difficult dialogue, while remaining mindful that our words can have real-world consequences.

8. The Role of Moderation

Our goal is to maintain a safe and stable environment for all participants. Because:

  • Context is often complex and incomplete
  • True consent is not always easy to verify
  • Safety requires proactive care

Moderation decisions prioritize: Protecting participants, Reducing risk, and Preventing escalation into harm.

The Complexity of Choice

We value individual freedom, but we also recognize its limits in practice. To build a healthy system, we have to consider what happens when theories meet reality.

For instance, we often discuss the boundaries of "full consent." In theory, if individuals agree to something, it should be their choice. In practice, we know that internal pressures, hidden information, and complex circumstances can make consent difficult to prove.

Because we cannot truly know every person's private context, we make a practical decision to prioritize clear boundaries and the safety of the community over abstract edge cases.

This means:

  • Moderators help navigate situations where safety is at risk.
  • We prioritize the stability of the space over testing theoretical limits.

This is a conscious choice. We are choosing a path that we believe is safer for everyone, even if it means we don't push every idea to its absolute extreme.

Our focus is on creating a space where everyone can participate without fear, and where responsibility to one another is as important as individual freedom.

Thank you for being part of this.

The Garden is intended as a sanctuary for thought and reflection. We hope that real-world conflicts are resolved peacefully elsewhere, allowing this space to remain focused on dialogue and shared discovery.
